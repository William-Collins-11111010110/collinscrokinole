⭐ Elo Rating System

Starting rating: Every player begins at 1500. This includes new players like Oakes, who has no game history yet.

Match-level updates (not per-game): Bo3/Bo5/Bo7 matches are grouped, and the expected score is calculated once using each player's rating before the match started — then applied uniformly to every individual game in that match. This means going 2-1 down before winning a match doesn't unfairly penalize the middle loss with an inflated post-win rating; every game in the set uses the same pre-match expectation.

Expected score formula:

E(A) = 1 / (1 + 10^((R_B − R_A) / 400))

Where R_A and R_B are the pre-match Elo ratings of the two players. This produces a probability between 0 and 1 — e.g. a 400-point rating gap means the favorite is expected to win about 91% of the time.

Rating change formula:

New Rating = Old Rating + K × (Actual Score − Expected Score)

Actual Score is 1 for a win, 0 for a loss, 0.5 for a tie. A player who wins when heavily favored gains very little; a player who wins as a big underdog gains a lot. Every match is zero-sum between the two players involved (what one gains, the other loses).

K-factor (how fast ratings move):

  • K = 20 flat for every game — swiss and Day 2/playoff games are weighted identically. (An earlier version gave Day 2 a K bump, but that pulled against the provisional-K logic below: uncertainty about a player's skill should go down, not up, by the time they reach Day 2, so stakes-based inflation was dropped in favor of one consistent value.)
  • K = 40 decaying linearly down to K=20 over a player's first tournament (however many games that turns out to be — 2 or 12) for new/provisional players. This isn't a flat game count: since 2022/2023 were elimination formats where a bad start meant a short debut, and 2024+ guarantees everyone a swiss floor, tying the taper to "however long your actual first tournament ran" is fairer than a fixed number that ignores tournament structure and format changes across years.

Leaderboard eligibility: A player doesn't appear on any family-wide leaderboard (Elo Leaderboard History, Peak Family Ranking's "eligible rounds" count, etc.) until their first tournament's year has fully concluded — not partway through it, no matter how many games they've played. This keeps a debuting player's still-settling rating from briefly (and misleadingly) appearing as the family's best, purely because the person who's actually best hasn't finished accumulating games yet. They rejoin the visible leaderboard starting with their second tournament.

Byes: Excluded entirely. A bye is a free win against no opponent, so it never moves anyone's rating.

Year-end regression: After each tournament year, every rating is pulled 10% of the way back toward 1500:

New Rating = Old Rating × 0.9 + 1500 × 0.1

This reflects that skill can genuinely change year to year, and prevents a past champion's rating from staying artificially inflated forever. Since only one Christmas Championship happens per year, the season boundary is fully known the moment a tournament's Final/Consolation concludes — so regression is applied immediately at that point, including after the most recent tournament. This means every "current Elo" shown on the site already reflects this adjustment, ready for the next season, rather than showing a temporary raw number that's waiting to be discounted later.

↓ Regression Snapshots (Elo Leaderboard History)

The Elo Leaderboard History tab shows an extra "↓ Regression → [year]" entry right after each year's Final/Consolation Final, including one after the most recent tournament. This shows everyone's rating exactly as adjusted for the season transition, before any of that next year's games have been played. It's purely a display convenience — it does not count as a "round" for Peak Family Ranking, eligible-rounds totals, or anything else on the site.

Championship Points

A separate, purely cumulative "career résumé" stat — unlike Elo, it never decays and reflects everything you've ever accomplished, not just current form. Every tournament finish (2022–2025) awards points on a fixed curve based on final position:

Why 5th–7th are flat: All three represent the same outcome — making the Day 2 cut and losing in the first bracket round (QF). There's no meaningful skill distinction between them, so they're rewarded equally.

Why there's a cliff at 8th: The drop from 40 to 22 points is deliberately steep (18 points — almost as large as the entire 8th→16th tail combined) to reward simply qualifying for Day 2 over posting a marginally better Swiss record but missing the cut. This curve is applied identically across all 4 years, even though 2022–2023 didn't have an identical Day 2 structure, for consistency.

Doubles results use a separate points system entirely (see "Summer Classic" card below) — the two never mix into one combined total.

Summer Classic (Doubles) — Format & Championship Points

Format: 8 teams, 3 rounds of Bo1 swiss, top 4 advance to a Bo3 playoff bracket (SF: 1v4 and 2v3, then a Final). No scores are tracked anywhere — just win/loss per game, and no point-differential data exists for doubles matches.

Individual tracking: Stats (Elo, H2H, records, Championship Points) are attributed per player, not per team, since doubles partners rotate year to year. Both members of a team receive the same Elo change and same win/loss credit from every match they play together. Elo uses the same engine as singles (flat K=20, 5-game provisional decay, year-end regression), computed entirely independently from a player's singles Elo.

Strength of Schedule (SOS / Opponent Win%)

A weighted average of your opponents' career win rates, weighted by how many games you actually played against each one. This answers: "on average, how good were the players you faced?"

SOS = Σ(Opponent's Win% × Games vs that Opponent) / Total Games Played

The "per-year" SOS shown in the season results table only uses that specific year's opponent win rates (not their career win rate), so it reflects the field strength for that particular tournament.

Doubles Team Balancing

All 16 players are ranked by current Elo rating, then paired using snake seeding: rank 1 pairs with rank 16, rank 2 with rank 15, rank 3 with rank 14, and so on down to rank 8 pairing with rank 9. This puts the strongest player on the same team as the weakest, aiming to make every team's combined average rating as close as possible.

Oakes (no game history) is excluded from doubles team generation since the original doubles format was built for the 16-player league before Oakes joined.

General Stat Definitions

Win%: (Wins + 0.5 × Ties) / Total Games × 100

+/− (Differential): Total points scored minus total points allowed, across all games.

Peak Elo: The highest Elo rating a player has ever reached at any single point in their history, even if their current rating has since dropped.

Greatest Win: Among all of a player's wins, the one against the highest-rated opponent (using that opponent's Elo at the time of the match, not their current rating).

Upsets vs Expected Mismatches (Records tab): Both are ranked by the Elo gap between the two players in a match. An "upset" is when the lower-rated player won; an "expected mismatch" is when the higher-rated player won as predicted, ranked by how big that predicted gap was.

Badges & Achievements (Singles)

Every player's profile has two related but different systems, shown at the bottom of their Singles tab:

Badges are one-time, earned-or-not accomplishments — either you've done the thing or you haven't. Most describe a single moment (a specific game or series), not a career total.

Achievements are tiered tracks built on a career-total stat. Almost all of them now scale infinitely: instead of capping at a fixed "Level 3 / MAXED," each one just keeps adding another star forever as the underlying number grows, using a start value and a repeating step (e.g. New Heights starts rewarding a level at 1550 peak Elo, then again every +50 after that, with no ceiling). The one exception is Cleared the Table (unique opponents beaten), which stays capped at a fixed 3 levels since there's a genuine ceiling — you can't beat more opponents than exist in the league.

Hall of Records

The Hall of Records page shows a top-3 podium for nine career/season categories, plus full detail tables for Greatest Upsets and Greatest Blowouts. Most are self-explanatory from the stats above, but two are worth spelling out:

Doubles Elo Leader: ranks the 8 Summer Classic teams (not individual players) by team Elo — a separate leaderboard from everything else on the page, which is otherwise about individual players.

Odds Defying: for every player-year, their Elo rating at the very start of that year (before any games that year) is used to rank the whole active field into a "predicted finish order." That's compared against where they actually finished. The biggest gap between predicted rank and actual finish wins — e.g. a player predicted to finish 16th out of 16 by pre-season Elo, who then actually finished 5th, would top this category by a wide margin.

All nine podium categories use the same underlying stats documented elsewhere on this page (Elo, Championship Points, career wins, etc.) — Odds Defying is the only one with genuinely new methodology.

⚠️ Data Notes & Exclusions
  • Maggie/Reese (a combined one-off substitute pairing from 2023) is included in the Full Game History tab for completeness, but excluded from every other stat, ranking, and the Elo system, since the results can't be attributed to a single player.
  • "Grandma" is merged into Kathy, and "Papa" is merged into Bob across all years — these were the same people, just referred to differently in different years' notes.
  • 2022 & 2023 used a different tournament format (swiss double/triple-elimination with a differential tiebreak, byes awarded to the best player rather than lowest seed) compared to 2024/2025's swiss-into-bracket structure. Head-to-head results are still fully accurate; only the sense of "Day 1 vs Day 2" doesn't cleanly apply to those years.
  • The 2024 Bronze medal match between William and Tim had its exact scores lost to memory — only the win/loss sequence was known (William, Tim, William), so placeholder scores (30-20, 15-25, 40-30) were used to preserve the correct match outcome without fabricating precise point totals.
